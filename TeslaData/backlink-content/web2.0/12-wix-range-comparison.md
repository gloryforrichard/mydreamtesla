# Tesla Range Compared: Which Model Goes the Farthest on a Single Charge?

Range is the number-one concern for anyone considering an electric vehicle. It's the first spec most buyers look up, the most frequently discussed topic in EV forums, and the primary source of what the industry has dubbed "range anxiety." For Tesla buyers specifically, the question usually isn't whether a Tesla has enough range — it's which Tesla has the *most* range and which configuration gets you the best balance of range versus price.

Let's break it all down. Every current Tesla model, every variant, and the real-world factors that make EPA numbers only part of the story.

## The EPA Range Lineup: Where Every Tesla Stands

Here's the current Tesla lineup ranked by EPA-estimated range, from highest to lowest. These are the official numbers from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency:

**Model S Long Range**: ~405 miles
**Model S Plaid**: ~350 miles
**Model 3 Long Range RWD**: ~363 miles
**Model 3 Long Range AWD**: ~341 miles
**Cybertruck AWD (with Range Extender)**: ~440+ miles*
**Cybertruck AWD**: ~318 miles
**Model X Long Range**: ~335 miles
**Model Y Long Range RWD**: ~325 miles
**Model Y Long Range AWD**: ~310 miles
**Cybertruck Cyberbeast**: ~301 miles
**Model X Plaid**: ~290 miles
**Model 3 RWD (Standard Range)**: ~272 miles
**Model Y RWD**: ~260 miles
**Cybertruck RWD**: ~250 miles

*The Cybertruck Range Extender is an optional battery pack that installs in the truck bed.

A few things jump out from this list. The Model S Long Range is Tesla's range king among standard configurations. The Model 3 Long Range RWD actually beats several more expensive models. And the range spread across the entire lineup — from 250 miles to 440+ miles — is enormous. Your choice of model and variant can mean the difference between charging once a week or once every two weeks for a typical commuter.

## Why RWD Models Get More Range Than AWD

You might notice a pattern: rear-wheel drive variants consistently outrange their all-wheel drive counterparts, sometimes by 30-50 miles. This seems counterintuitive — shouldn't four-wheel drive be more efficient since each motor does less work?

In practice, a single rear motor is more efficient than two motors working together. Here's why:

1. **Fewer drivetrain losses.** A single motor has less mechanical friction and fewer components generating heat than a dual-motor setup.
2. **Motor efficiency curves.** Electric motors have a "sweet spot" of RPM and torque where they operate most efficiently. A single motor can be optimized for the highway cruising speeds where most range testing occurs.
3. **Weight.** AWD models are heavier — the front motor, additional half-shafts, and related components add 150-250 lbs. More weight means more energy needed to maintain speed.

This doesn't mean AWD is the wrong choice. In snow, rain, or any low-traction condition, AWD provides significantly better grip and safety. The acceleration advantage of AWD is substantial too. But if pure range is your priority and you live in a mild climate, RWD is the empirically better choice.

## EPA Range vs. Real-World Range: The Gap

Let's address the elephant in the room: you almost certainly won't achieve the EPA range number in normal driving.

The EPA testing cycle is conducted at an average speed of about 48 mph, with a mix of city and highway driving, in moderate temperature, with minimal accessory use. Real-world driving looks different:

**Highway speed matters enormously.** Aerodynamic drag increases with the square of velocity. Driving at 80 mph instead of 65 mph can reduce your range by 20-25%. A Model 3 Long Range rated at 363 miles might deliver 280-300 miles at sustained highway speeds.

**Temperature is the second biggest factor.** Cold weather is the range killer. At 20 degrees Fahrenheit, expect to lose 25-35% of your rated range due to battery heating requirements, cabin heating, increased rolling resistance from cold tires, and reduced battery chemical efficiency. A 350-mile rated car might deliver 230-250 miles in a cold Midwestern winter.

Hot weather also reduces range, though less dramatically. Running the A/C at full blast in Arizona summer might cost you 10-15% versus a mild spring day.

**Other real-world drains:**

- **Elevation changes.** Climbing a mountain pass burns energy fast. (You get some back on the descent through regenerative braking, but it's never 1:1.)
- **Wind.** A strong headwind can reduce range more than most people expect — 10-15% in a sustained 20+ mph headwind.
- **Tire type and pressure.** All-season tires are less efficient than the low-rolling-resistance tires Tesla ships from the factory. Under-inflated tires add rolling resistance.
- **Cargo and passengers.** A fully loaded car with four passengers and luggage uses more energy than a solo driver.

## Model-by-Model Range Analysis

### Model 3: The Efficiency Champion

The Model 3 is Tesla's most efficient vehicle in terms of energy consumed per mile. The RWD variant achieves roughly 25 kWh per 100 miles, which is remarkably low for a full-size sedan. This efficiency is why the Model 3 punches above its battery size in range comparisons — it doesn't have the largest battery in the Tesla lineup, but it does the most with every kilowatt-hour.

The Highland update (2024+) improved efficiency further through better aerodynamics, an updated heat pump system, and refined motor control software. The result is the longest-range Model 3 ever despite no significant increase in battery capacity.

### Model Y: The Practical Middle Ground

The Model Y shares its platform with the Model 3 but sits taller and has a larger frontal area, which hurts aerodynamics. Expect roughly 10-15% less range than an equivalent Model 3 configuration. However, the Model Y's range is still excellent for its class — no competitor crossover EV matches it at a comparable price point.

For families using the Model Y as their primary vehicle, the Long Range AWD variant at ~310 miles is the sweet spot. It provides enough range for daily driving plus weekend trips without the constant need to plan around charging.

### Model S: The Range King

The Model S Long Range sits atop the range chart at ~405 miles. The larger battery pack and exceptional aerodynamics (drag coefficient of just 0.208) combine to deliver the most miles per charge of any Tesla without additional accessories.

The Plaid variant trades about 55 miles of range for its insane tri-motor performance. The energy demands of three motors and the Plaid's tendency to inspire spirited driving mean real-world Plaid range often lands around 280-300 miles.

### Model X: Trading Range for Space

The Model X is the least efficient Tesla per mile, which is expected for the largest and heaviest passenger vehicle in the lineup. The falcon-wing doors, while practical for passenger loading, create aerodynamic compromises. The Long Range variant at ~335 miles is respectable for a vehicle this size, but real-world highway range often sits around 260-280 miles.

### Cybertruck: The Wild Card

The Cybertruck's range numbers are harder to pin down because it's the most affected by real-world conditions. Its flat-panel design creates more aerodynamic drag than Tesla's other vehicles, and its curb weight of nearly 7,000 lbs means more energy is needed for acceleration and hill climbing.

The Range Extender battery pack — which sits in the truck bed — pushes the AWD variant past 440 miles, but at the cost of reduced bed space and an additional several thousand dollars. For most Cybertruck owners, the standard AWD range of ~318 miles covers daily driving needs comfortably.

## Charging Speed: The Other Half of the Range Equation

Range isn't just about how far you can go on a charge — it's also about how quickly you can add miles when you stop. Tesla's Supercharger network (V3 and V4 stations) delivers peak charging speeds that vary by model:

- **Model 3/Y**: Up to 250 kW peak, adding roughly 175 miles in 15 minutes
- **Model S/X**: Up to 250 kW peak, adding roughly 200 miles in 15 minutes
- **Cybertruck**: Up to 250 kW peak, adding roughly 135 miles in 15 minutes

Charging speed decreases as the battery fills (a phenomenon called "tapering"), so the most efficient strategy is to charge from 10-80% and then get back on the road. Charging from 80-100% is dramatically slower and generally not worth the time during road trips.

## Making Range Work for You

The key insight from all of this data is that the "best" range depends entirely on your use case:

- **Daily commuters** (under 50 miles/day) will be fine with any Tesla, including the shortest-range options. Just plug in at home each night.
- **Mixed use drivers** who want to avoid range anxiety should target 300+ miles of rated range, which means a Long Range variant of any model.
- **Frequent road trippers** should prioritize both range and charging speed — the Model S Long Range is the optimal choice here.
- **Cold climate drivers** should factor in a 25-35% winter range loss and choose accordingly.
