# My Journey Into Electric: Why I Spent Months Researching Tesla Models

I never thought I'd be a car person. For most of my adult life, a car was just a thing that got me from point A to point B. I drove a series of forgettable sedans, filled them with gas when the light came on, and didn't think much about it. Cars were appliances.

Then, about two years ago, a friend let me drive his Model 3. I pulled out of his driveway, pressed the accelerator, and the back of my seat tried to swallow me whole. By the time I merged onto the highway, something had fundamentally shifted. Not just because of the speed — though that was absurd for a sedan that cost less than $50K — but because the whole experience felt like driving something from ten years in the future. No engine noise, no gear shifts, no vibration. Just smooth, instant, silent thrust.

I handed the keys back and went home. By midnight, I was three hours deep into Tesla forums, YouTube reviews, and spec sheets. That was the beginning of a research rabbit hole that consumed the better part of four months.

## The Problem With Researching Teslas

Here's what I discovered quickly: comparing Tesla models is surprisingly complicated.

It's not like comparing, say, a Honda Civic to a Toyota Corolla, where each model has a couple of trim levels that barely change year over year. Tesla has five distinct models, each with multiple variants, and each model has gone through generational updates that significantly change the specs. A 2018 Model 3 Long Range and a 2024 Model 3 Long Range share a name but differ in range, acceleration, interior design, software, and driving feel.

I tried keeping track in spreadsheets. I had tabs for each model, columns for range, acceleration, price, dimensions, cargo space, weight, charging speed, and a dozen other specs I learned to care about. The data was scattered across Tesla's own website (which doesn't make it easy to compare across generations), press releases, EPA filings, owner forums, and YouTube reviewers who each tested under different conditions.

Some specific frustrations:

- Tesla's website only shows current models. Want to compare a 2021 Model Y to a 2024 Model Y? You'll need a third-party source.
- Range numbers vary between EPA estimates, Tesla's own "estimated range" display, and real-world results at highway speed.
- Pricing history is hard to track — Tesla changes prices frequently, sometimes multiple times in a quarter.
- Certain specs (like curb weight, cargo dimensions, or ground clearance) are buried or inconsistent across sources.

I wasn't the only one struggling. On r/TeslaMotors and various owner forums, I saw the same questions repeated weekly: "What changed between the 2021 and 2023 Model Y?" "Is the Model S Plaid worth it over the Long Range?" "How much range do you really lose in winter?"

## Down the Rabbit Hole

What started as "which Tesla should I buy?" expanded into a genuine fascination with how these vehicles had evolved. I found myself comparing the original 2012 Model S — with its black plastic nosecone and 265-mile range — to the current Plaid variant doing 0-60 in under two seconds. The engineering progression was remarkable.

I became the person in my friend group who could rattle off the difference between a Model Y Performance and a Model X Long Range, or explain why the Highland Model 3's range was better despite having a similar battery capacity to its predecessor (aerodynamics, drivetrain efficiency, heat pump improvements). My partner's eyes glazed over at dinner. My coworkers started routing their EV questions to me.

The more I learned, the more I wished there was a single place that had all of this information organized cleanly. A tool where you could select two or three Tesla variants from any generation and see every spec side by side — not a YouTube video where someone reads numbers off a screen for twenty minutes, but a clean, interactive comparison.

## Building What I Couldn't Find

Eventually, I realized that if this tool didn't exist in the form I wanted, I could build it. I'm a developer by trade, and I had already done the hardest part — compiling the data.

That frustration became [mydreamtesla.com](https://mydreamtesla.com).

The core idea is simple: take every Tesla model, across every generation and variant, and let users compare them side by side with 50+ specs per vehicle. Range, acceleration, dimensions, weight, cargo space, pricing, charging speeds, motor configurations — everything a buyer (or an obsessive researcher like me) would want to see in one place.

Building it forced me to organize all the data I'd been hoarding in messy spreadsheets into a proper database. Every number was verified against official sources — EPA filings, Tesla's press kits, and measured specs from owners. Where sources conflicted, I noted the discrepancy. The goal was accuracy above everything.

The process taught me things about Tesla's lineup that surprised me even after months of research. For example, how the Model 3 Long Range RWD actually has more range than the AWD version because a single motor is more efficient at highway speeds. Or how the Model X, despite being Tesla's largest vehicle, has a lower drag coefficient than you'd expect thanks to its tapered rear design.

## What I Actually Bought

After all that research, I bought a Model Y Long Range AWD. It wasn't the fastest, it wasn't the longest-range, and it certainly wasn't the most exciting choice. But it was the right choice for my actual life — a mix of daily commuting, weekend trips with my partner, and the occasional furniture haul that makes a sedan impractical.

The research process itself became more valuable than I expected, though. Not just because it led to building a tool that other people now use, but because it changed how I think about purchasing decisions in general. When you actually understand the specs — not just the marketing highlights, but the real numbers — you make better choices. You stop comparing sticker prices and start comparing cost per mile. You stop caring about 0-60 times you'll never actually use and start caring about cargo volume and charging speed.

## The Ongoing Journey

I still update the data on mydreamtesla.com whenever Tesla releases changes. The recent Cybertruck specs were a challenge — it's such a departure from the rest of the lineup that I had to add entirely new spec categories. The Model 3 Highland required updating the comparison framework to properly show what changed versus the pre-Highland version.

If you're at the beginning of your own Tesla research journey, I have one piece of advice: don't trust any single source. Look at the actual numbers, put them side by side, and make the decision based on what matters to your daily life. The "best" Tesla doesn't exist in the abstract — it only exists relative to how you'll actually use it.

And if you want to skip the four-month spreadsheet phase I went through, well, that's why I built the tool in the first place.
